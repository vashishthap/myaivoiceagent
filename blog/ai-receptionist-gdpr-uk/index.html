Are AI receptionists GDPR-compliant in the UK?

The short answer is yes — if you (and your vendor) do five specific things. The longer answer is the one that matters before you sign anything: where the audio actually goes, how long it lives, what your callers are told, and what you've got in writing. This guide walks through each piece in plain English, then gives you an 8-question checklist you can send any AI vendor before signing.

This is general guidance for UK SMB owners, not legal advice. If you process special category data (health, biometrics) at scale, get a UK data protection lawyer or your DPO to review your specific setup.

Short answer (with caveats)

A UK AI receptionist is GDPR-compliant when five things are true at the same time:

  • You have a valid lawful basis under UK GDPR Article 6 — usually legitimate interests for inbound business calls, or contract performance for booked appointments.
  • If the call touches health, biometrics, or other special category data, you also have an Article 9 condition — for healthcare contexts, that's usually 9(2)(h) (provision of healthcare).
  • You and your vendor have a signed Data Processing Agreement (DPA) under Article 28 covering security, sub-processors, breach notification, and deletion.
  • The processing is transparent: callers know they're talking to an AI and that the call is recorded, and your privacy notice covers AI processing.
  • The data is kept only as long as needed and stored somewhere you can defend — ideally UK or EU infrastructure, or somewhere with appropriate transfer safeguards.

Get those five right and you're on solid ground. Get any one wrong and you're potentially a breach away from a notifiable incident. The rest of this article walks through each one in enough detail to ask the right questions of any vendor — and to defend your choice if the ICO ever comes knocking.

Where call data lives

An AI receptionist call is not a single thing happening in one place. It's a chain of services, each one potentially in a different country:

  1. Telephony bridge (typically Twilio or similar) carries the audio from your UK number to the AI platform. Twilio operates UK and EU regions; whether your vendor uses them depends on configuration.
  2. Speech-to-text turns the caller's audio into text. Provider varies — Deepgram, Whisper (run on Azure or AWS), Google STT.
  3. Large language model decides what to say next. Often OpenAI, Anthropic, or a hosted open-source model. Each has different data-residency options and zero-retention contracts.
  4. Text-to-speech generates the reply audio (commonly ElevenLabs, with EU residency available on enterprise plans).
  5. Calendar / CRM integration writes the booking back to Google Calendar, Dentally, EXACT, or your CRM — whose data residency depends on that platform.
  6. Call recording & transcript storage — usually the AI vendor's own database. This is where retention policy bites.

A vendor who can show you a clean diagram of all six legs — with the data centre region for each — is doing this seriously. A vendor who can't answer the question is the answer. For UK or EU residency, ask specifically: “Is every leg of the call — STT, LLM, TTS, storage — processed in the UK or EU, or are any sub-processors based outside?”

US-based sub-processors aren't automatically a dealbreaker. The UK Government's data adequacy bridge with the EU and the UK extension to the EU–US Data Privacy Framework (in force since October 2023) cover most major US providers as long as the provider has self-certified. But you need to know the routing, document it in your record of processing activities (Article 30), and reflect it in your privacy notice.

Retention and erasure

UK GDPR doesn't set a fixed retention period for call data. It tells you to set one yourself, document it, and stick to it. The principle is data minimisation: keep only what you genuinely need, only as long as you need it.

Sensible defaults for most UK SMB use cases in 2026:

  • Audio recordings: 30 to 90 days for quality assurance, then auto-delete. Keep specific calls longer only if you have a clear reason (a complaint, a disputed booking, a safeguarding concern).
  • Transcripts: 6 to 12 months. Useful for training and audit; far less sensitive than audio because identifiers can be redacted.
  • Booking metadata (name, contact number, appointment time): for as long as you need it for the underlying business reason — i.e. the appointment's lifecycle plus any legal limitation period.
  • Healthcare clinical records: governed by your clinical record-keeping rules, not your AI platform's retention policy. Keep clinical detail in your PMS (Dentally, EXACT, SOE), not in the AI vendor's database.

Erasure (the right to be forgotten under Article 17) needs to actually work. Ask your vendor: “If a caller exercises their right to erasure tomorrow, can you delete their audio, transcript, and metadata across every system within 30 days, including any backups?” If the answer is hand-wavy, it's a problem.

Practical tip. Build retention into your AI vendor configuration on day one — auto-delete after a set window. Catching up retroactively after a year of accumulated audio is painful and often impossible at the backup layer.

Consent on the call

“Consent” is the most misused word in this whole conversation. Under UK GDPR, consent is a specific, narrow lawful basis: it must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous, and it must be as easy to withdraw as to give. For most inbound business calls you do not need, and should not rely on, consent. You'll typically rely on:

  • Legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f)) for general inbound enquiries, FAQ handling, and lead qualification — with a documented Legitimate Interests Assessment (LIA) on file.
  • Contract performance (Article 6(1)(b)) for processing necessary to take or manage a booking the caller is requesting.
  • Article 9(2)(h) for healthcare-related processing (dental triage, medical receptionists), under the responsibility of a healthcare professional bound by professional secrecy.

What you do need on every call — whether you call it consent or simply transparency — is a clear opening disclosure. The minimum is that the caller knows two things: they're speaking to an AI, and the call may be recorded. A single line at the start handles both. The ICO's guidance on call recording is the relevant reference here, and reasonable practice is well-established.

If you process EU residents' data, the EU AI Act's Article 50 (transparency obligations for AI systems interacting with people) applies from August 2026. UK businesses serving EU customers should treat AI disclosure as a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have.

What to ask any AI vendor (8-question checklist)

Send this to every vendor on your shortlist. Their answers tell you more about whether they take compliance seriously than any glossy security page. A serious vendor will reply within a few business days; a hand-wavy reply is the answer.

  1. What data residency do you offer? Specifically: where are STT, LLM, TTS, audio storage, and transcript storage processed? Can you provide a data flow diagram?
  2. Do you sign a UK GDPR-compliant Data Processing Agreement (Article 28)? Please share your standard DPA for review.
  3. Who are your sub-processors, and is there a public list with notification of changes? (Required under most DPAs.)
  4. What's your standard retention period for call audio, transcripts, and metadata, and can it be configured per-customer?
  5. How do you handle data subject rights — access, rectification, erasure, portability? What's the SLA, and does erasure cover backups?
  6. What security certifications do you hold (ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, Cyber Essentials Plus)? Are your sub-processors equivalently certified?
  7. What's your breach notification process — how quickly will you tell us, and what information will you provide so we can meet our 72-hour ICO obligation?
  8. Are call recordings used to train any AI model, yours or a sub-processor's? If yes, on what basis — and can we opt out?

The eighth question is where most vendors get nervous. Read the answer carefully. If your callers' recordings are being used to train a third-party model without a clear lawful basis and explicit contractual carve-out, that is a problem you don't want on your record of processing activities.

What to do next

If you're evaluating AI receptionists for a UK business, the practical next steps are short:

  1. Send the 8-question checklist to every vendor on your shortlist. Compare answers side by side.
  2. Ask for sample DPAs from your top two and forward them to your DPO or solicitor for review — reading two contracts costs less than one ICO penalty.
  3. Run a short Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) before going live. The ICO has a free template; it takes a couple of hours and gives you a paper trail if anything is ever questioned.
  4. Update your privacy notice and website cookie/AI disclosure to reflect that calls are answered by an AI assistant, what data is processed, where it goes, and how long it's kept.
  5. Configure retention on day one. Don't accept a default that keeps audio for “as long as needed” with no end date.

Done in that order, you end up with a defensible record of processing, a vendor who's on the hook in writing, and an AI receptionist that does the job without quietly creating a compliance problem in the background.

Frequently asked questions

Is using an AI receptionist legal under UK GDPR?

Yes, in principle, provided you have a valid lawful basis, a clear purpose, a proportionate retention period, and a signed Data Processing Agreement with your vendor. Healthcare contexts also need an Article 9 condition such as 9(2)(h).

Do I have to tell callers they're speaking to an AI?

No specific UK law mandates it in May 2026, but the ICO's transparency principle and the EU AI Act both push strongly toward disclosure. A single opening line covering “you're through to our AI assistant, your call may be recorded” is current best practice.

Where does my AI call data go?

Vendor-dependent. Ask for a data flow diagram covering STT, LLM, TTS, and storage. UK/EU residency is achievable; US sub-processors are allowed under the UK–US Data Privacy Framework but must be documented.

How long can I keep AI call recordings?

As long as you need them, then no longer. 30–90 days for audio and 6–12 months for transcripts are common defaults. Document your rationale in a retention schedule.

Why do I need a DPA?

UK GDPR Article 28 makes a written Data Processing Agreement mandatory between you (controller) and your vendor (processor). Without one you're non-compliant regardless of how secure the underlying tech is.
