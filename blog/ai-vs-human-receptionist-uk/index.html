AI vs human receptionist: a UK SMB cost breakdown

The headline question we get from UK small business owners is short: do I replace my receptionist with an AI? The honest answer is almost never a clean yes or no. Below is the cost breakdown — with every line item a UK SMB actually pays — followed by a framework for choosing AI, human, or both.

The headline numbers

Three figures cover 90% of UK SMB reality in May 2026.

  • AI receptionist (done-for-you): £200–£500 per month, plus a one-time setup of £500–£1,000. Covers 24/7 inbound calling with a defined call cap and inclusive integrations.
  • Part-time in-house receptionist (20 hours/week): £14,000–£18,000 a year fully loaded — salary plus employer NI, pension auto-enrolment, paid holiday, sick cover, and a share of training and equipment. Covers about 4 hours a day on the phone, 5 days a week.
  • Full-time in-house receptionist (37.5 hours/week): £28,000–£36,000 a year fully loaded. Covers normal business hours, 5 days a week — not evenings, not weekends, not bank holidays without overtime or shift cover.

AI is roughly 10–15× cheaper than a full-time human on a pure cost basis, and 5–7× cheaper than a part-time hire. The interesting question isn't which is cheaper. It's which actually answers the calls you need answered, when you need them answered.

£200/mo vs £25k/year + on-costs

The annual comparison hides where the human cost actually sits. ONS median pay for SOC 4216 (receptionists) in late 2025 was approximately £24,000–£26,000 for full-time UK roles, with the spread heavily skewed by location (London +20%, Northern England −10%). That's only the visible line, though. To get to a true loaded cost you need to add on the things that don't appear on the payslip.

Net: about £24,000–£30,000 of annual savings on the cost line if AI fully replaces a full-time receptionist, or about £10,000–£14,000 if AI replaces a part-time one. That's real money. But it only matters if the AI handles the calls the human was handling.

Two costs people forget when running this comparison. First, management overhead — every direct-employed receptionist costs you somewhere between two and five hours a month of practice-manager or owner time on rota changes, performance conversations, holiday cover, and the inevitable HR friction. At a £40–£80/hour blended owner rate, that's another £100–£400 a month you don't see on the payslip. AI removes this entirely. Second, capacity ceilings — a human receptionist who is on the phone with one caller cannot answer the second line. UK SMBs running a single receptionist routinely lose 8–15% of calls to "engaged" tones during peak hours. AI handles concurrent calls on the same line, so the second caller's experience is identical to the first's.

Where humans still win (judgement, complaints, hand-off)

We sell AI receptionists for a living and we still tell customers: don't expect AI to do everything a great human receptionist does. There are three call types where humans remain materially better in 2026, and being honest about them is the only way to get a hybrid setup right.

Complex complaints. A patient calling about a billing dispute after a botched referral, or a diner who reported food poisoning last week — these calls reward judgement, sincere apology, and an instinct for when to escalate. AI can take tier-1 complaints fine ("my appointment was cancelled and I need to rebook") but tier-2 and tier-3 escalations need a person who can deviate from script, offer compensation, and know when to put the practice manager on. We always configure a clear escalation path for these.

Open-ended professional conversations. A solicitor's bereavement-stricken caller, a private clinic's anxious patient asking unscripted questions, a tradesperson's first-time customer who wants to chat about an unusual job — these calls aren't transactional and don't end with a calendar booking. They want a person who can listen, reassure, and improvise. AI handles them, but most owners we work with prefer a human voice on these, and we route accordingly.

Clean hand-offs and warm transfers. If a caller needs to speak to you specifically — because you're the gas-safe engineer, the solicitor, the senior partner — a human receptionist can warm-transfer the call ("I have John on the line about the boiler quote you sent yesterday"). AI can do this too, but the warmth and context of a quick human briefing still wins for high-value relationship calls.

The honest summary: if 30%+ of your monthly inbound calls fall into one of these three buckets, replacing your receptionist outright is the wrong move. Augmenting them is the right one.

Hybrid: AI front door + human escalation

The model that's emerged across our UK customer base in 2026 — and the one we recommend most often — is hybrid, not replacement. The shape is:

  • AI takes every inbound call first. It greets in your brand voice, qualifies, and books 80–90% of routine calls end-to-end. The receptionist (or owner) doesn't see those calls at all unless they want to.
  • The AI routes the rest. Trigger phrases ("I want to speak to a person", "I'd like to make a complaint", "this is urgent"), specific topic flags (NHS triage red flags, complaints, high-value sales keywords), and after-hours emergency routes all push the call to a human — either your existing receptionist on shift, a smaller answering-service package, or your mobile.
  • The receptionist's role shifts. Less time on routine bookings, more time on the high-value and emotional calls that genuinely need a human. Job satisfaction usually goes up; turnover often goes down.

Financially, the hybrid model usually means keeping a part-time human and adding AI on top — total spend in the £18,000–£24,000 range a year (£12,000–£16,000 part-time human + £3,000–£6,000 AI), but covering 24/7 instead of 5×8. For most UK SMBs that's a better ratio than either pure option.

It's also the model that protects against a year-1 AI mistake. If the agent occasionally fumbles a tricky call in the first six weeks (it will — every setup needs tuning), a human is there to catch it before it becomes a lost booking or a Google review.

One nuance worth flagging: the hybrid model works best when the human and the AI share the same record. If your receptionist is still using a paper diary and your AI is writing into Dentally or HubSpot, you'll end up with split records and the human will resent the AI by month two. On Pro and Premier plans we configure the AI and the receptionist's UI to point at the same calendar and CRM, so a booking made by either lands in the same place. It's a small detail that decides whether the hybrid lasts a quarter or a decade.

Decision framework: 3 questions

Three honest questions narrow the decision in under two minutes. Answer them based on your last 30 days of calls.

1. How many inbound calls do you take in a month? Under 50 → keep a part-time human or use a light answering service; AI flat-fee is overkill at that volume. 50–500 → AI clearly wins on cost; hybrid wins on quality. 500+ → AI is the only economically sensible front door; layer a human on the escalation path.

2. What proportion of those calls follow a repeatable pattern (booking, FAQ, lead qualification)? 70%+ → AI handles the lion's share well. 50–70% → AI handles the routine, human handles the rest. Below 50% → keep the human in front; consider AI only as a 24/7 overflow agent.

3. Are you currently missing calls outside hours? Look at your call log. If callers reach voicemail in the evening or at weekends and don't call back, you're losing bookings every week that wouldn't cost AI a penny extra to catch. A 5-minute look at our missed-call cost calculator usually puts a £/month figure on the lost revenue — and that figure is almost always larger than the AI plan.

One more honest note: if your current receptionist is excellent and stays for years, the financial gap narrows. A loyal, brilliant receptionist who knows every regular by name is genuinely hard to replicate in software in 2026. The case for hybrid (rather than replacement) is strongest here — the AI takes the routine load off, your excellent receptionist keeps doing the work she's actually best at, and you stop losing the out-of-hours volume to voicemail.
